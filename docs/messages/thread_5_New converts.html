From: Judi Lapsley Miller | Date: 2004-08-02 10:18:21 UTC
Yippee!!! Big happy dance here!!! Finally some more kiwi boxers :-DDD

Don't worry about how good your carving is or isn't - it's just a matter of
doing it - you'll be surprised what you can do after a little practice and
experimentation. (yeah I need to listen to my own advice - I haven't carved
for ages and am scared to get back into it...).

There are lots of techniques to try out and there are some very good
tutorials on the web (e.g., http://atlasquest.com/tutorials/carving/)
I started off with an old Mars eraser and a small box-cutter and got
reasonable results. I now use a gouging blade (that linoleum carvers use)
and am experimenting with cannula needles (that are used for body-piercing!)
to get fine detail. I'm not sure what supplies are available in NZ - Linton
and I worked for 4 years in the USA (where we got the letterboxing bug) and
came back to NZ only last October so we're still scoping things out. I've
noticed that stamp pads and erasers are very expensive here - I brought back
a stash thank goodness. There is a Stamp/Scrapbooking shop in Wellington
which may have some other carving supplies - I'll check them out sometime.
Otherwise I'm tempted to order supplies through the internet when I run out.

Some various thoughts on making up letterboxes... I've put a stamp pad in
most boxes I've placed, but I'm thinking I might not do that in future. In
the states there are regional differences as to whether stamp pads are
included or not - generally weather dictated. In Dartmoor (the home of
letterboxing in the UK) there is a big problem of the stamp pads being
stolen. Here I'm inclined not to because of the expense and also because the
best containers I've found so far are those 200ml "sistema" clear containers
(with blue click-clack locks) from the supermarket - they are a bit small to
put a logbook, stamp, and stamp pad in comfortably. In the USA, many boxes
use spiral-bound notecards for the log book. I haven't seen similar here.
I've found small artist's books that would suit a larger container (but the
smaller the better). Unlined books are nicer b/c the stamp images come out
more clearly, but most little books I've found are lined. I make my own
logbooks, mainly b/c I like making them. I can then size them to the
container. Some "micro-boxes" just use a film cannister and a long skinny
roll of paper. Water-proofing is a big deal and it's a good idea to
double-bag the logbook (using ziplock plastic bags) and then even put the
container in another bag.

Hope some of this burble helps. We're happy to help and are delighted at the
prospect of new boxes to find :-))

Cheers,

Judi

> But it showed us that we are absolutely TERRIBLE at stamp
> carving!

Don't feel too bad if your first carving looks terrible. Very few
people can carve well their first time out! Once you try it a few
times, however, you'll be surprised at how well you can carve. It
generally doesn't take long before people are carving stamps they
feel are 'acceptable'. And with a bit more practice, you'll be
ooohh-ing and ahhh-ing your stamps in wonder! =) Really!

Getting supplies might be difficult, though. I kept my eyes open
for carving and stamp supplies while traveling around Central
America and couldn't find anything that was even remotely useful
except for containers. And because people there are SOO poor, I was
afraid if I used one of those containers for a box (they didn't have
the cheap, plastic containers frequently used in the United States--
they don't have the 'luxury' of having disposable items like that),
if any of the locals found it, they'd take it home with them to
actually use for food! (The gall! *smile*)

I've never been to New Zealand before, but from what I've heard,
poverty isn't quite the problem there as in Central America, so the
theft of Rubbermaid containers for use or sale probably isn't as
much of a concern. But I can see where getting carving supplies
might be difficult.

Maybe Judy--if you're up for it--can buy in bulk from the Internet
at times and be the 'local supply' for carving and stamping supplies
if nothing good is found. I'm not sure how practical that would be,
but it's a thought I'd throw out there. =)

Good luck! =)

-- Ryan

I haven't seen the Rubbermaid containers here but the ones I mentioned look
just as good, if not better. They actually have a rubber ring within the
lid, which should make the container more waterproof (but time will tell).
They're also smaller and so easier to hide. I bought one the other day for
NZ$2 (US$1.30) - the Rubbermaid ones used to cost me just under US$3. So NZ
wins on the container front :-)

I'm sure gouge blades are around - we have some lovely art supply stores. I
did find a complete carving set in one store, but I can't remember how much
it was. I haven't seen the large blocks of carving material though. Mars
erasers are expensive, but I did find some cheaper vinyl erasers that I
haven't tried to carve yet. I'm guessing they may be more crumbly.

Another option may be the Dartmoor way, where a hand-drawn image is taken to
a rubber-stamp maker and it is professionally made. There is such a store in
Wellington, but I have not investigated yet (mainly b/c I prefer carving the
images myself).
