We've thrilled to see a number of new letterboxers join the list recently, so I thought it might be a good time to make some general observations and suggestions about letterboxing. There is also a new letterboxing group especially for newbies - it has an American focus, but may help answer some of your questions
http://groups.yahoo.com/group/newboxers/.
As well as Linton and me here in NZ, there are also a number of other experienced boxers from overseas on this list that are happy to answer your questions.
 
The basics (which are mostly pretty obvious, but are worth saying just in case):
  • It's important to be discreet so as to not draw attention to the box location. If someone approachs you to ask what you're doing, it's usually easier to say you're taking photos, sketching a flower, or eating lunch. We usually wait until there is no-one about before approaching the box location - both when finding and when rehiding.
  • It may help to take the box a little ways away from the hiding spot to stamp in.
  • Rehide the box well - better than you found it if possible (though still in the same spot!). It's not a good idea to leave a little bit of the box showing to help the next person. A well-hidden box should never be found inadvertently.
  • Make sure all the plastic baggies are well sealed and that the log book particulary is protected from water. We have found too many letterboxes that have been trashed by the weather. We try to carry extra plastic bags just in case a box we find needs running repairs.
  • If you're planting a box, avoid sensitive areas (delicate plants, rare birds, sacred grounds, historic ruins).
  • It's OK to talk about specific letterboxes on the talk lists in very general terms (ie you found it, it needs repair, etc), but avoid giving away the location, or the answers to a mystery or cryptic clue, or any other surprises that there might be. If you're not sure about something it is best to email the box owner/maintainer first.
  • Be careful to not put yourself or others in a dangerous situation by ignoring fatigue, changing weather, etc., on the quest for a box. It is all to easy to get obsessed with the chase!
  • Be warned - letterboxing is addictive! But good for you too (how often does that happen these days!!??)
If you've been looking round some of the letterboxing sites and groups, you may wonder what P, F, and X counts are. P is for the number of boxes Placed, F for the number Found, and X for the number eXchanged with other boxers. One dude in Dartmoor, England has found over 50000 letterboxes (that's not a typo).  Although people keep these statistics, letterboxing isn't a competition.
 
I hope some of this is helpful. Feel free to email me with any questions if you don't want to ask the entire group.
 
Cheers,
 
Judi
