From: Judi Lapsley Miller | Date: 2004-11-13 22:51:56 UTC
Yes, Mastercarve is what I use. If you use the 3/4" blocks, rather than the
1/2" blocks they don't need mounting. That said, I tend to buy the
4"x4"x1/2" blocks because they work out the cheapest per square inch, and I
can usually get 2-4 stamps out of one block. I don't know if the shop has
got any in yet - I haven't run out of my stash yet! With the exchange rate
being so favourable to NZers now, I'd suggest ordering it in from the USA.

Here is a link to Mastercarve blocks at Dick Blick's (a USA art supply
store) which is the online shop I used when I lived in the USA:
http://www.dickblick.com/zz404/00/products.asp?param=0&ig_id=5195
Some folk like this stuff better, you can get finer detail but it's a bit
harder to use and generally needs mounting because its thin.
http://www.dickblick.com/zz404/02/products.asp?param=0&ig_id=1862
They say they ship overseas: "Outside contiguous U.S., except Alaska,
Hawaii, and Puerto Rico (see above), will be charged actual shipping
charges. Please call for charges: 1-800-828-4548 or 1-309-343-6181." but I'd
be tempted to use their web form first to see if I could save a toll call.

For carving, these handles are incredibly cheap
http://www.dickblick.com/zz229/30b/products.asp?param=0&ig_id=1846
and can be easier to use than the chunkier handlers
http://www.dickblick.com/zz402/01/products.asp?param=0&ig_id=1847
I'd suggest getting 2 or 3 - one for each type of blade you use.

The blades or cutters that are most useful for rubber stamps are
http://www.dickblick.com/zz402/03/products.asp?param=0&ig_id=1842
Blades 1 (liner), 2 (v-gouge), and 4 (u-gouge). You'll probably find
v-gouges the most useful. These blades can be sharpened with a sharpening
stone, but a lot of people will just use a new blade.

There is much discussion and debate over how best to mount stamps. You can
get the mounting blocks that are used by store-bought rubber stamps, but
they are expensive. Be careful using a soft or laminated wood like balsa or
plywood because they can warp if they get wet. If you're lucky enough to
have a carpenter friend, you may be able to get hold of hardwood cutoffs.
You also need to take into consideration the extra room taken up in the box
by the mount. I've occasionally mounted the stamp onto the lid of the stamp
pad, but again, work out if it will fit in the box before gluing!! Also,
some glues will eat into the rubber and some will soften when wet. I don't
have any recommendations sorry.

Cheers,

Judi

Erasers seem to last well b/c they're small. It depends on how many visitors
the box gets - I don't think we're in any danger of wearing stamps out just
yet :-))

One of the key things to stop a stamp from crumbling is to be careful not to
undercut the raised part of the image when you're carving. It's easy to
undercut when using a flat xacto blade, but the v-gouge blade ensures a good
angle.

Amy, this is a little late in reply, but my thoughts on mounting are
that the stamp is so much easier to use if mounted. We have been
getting scraps of wood from my parent's woodpile (we don't have much
call for one of those in our apartment!), sanding them down and then
polyurethaning them once we have put an image on it. We use foam to
cushion the stamp like you can get from a scrapbooking or card making
store. The one we have has adhesive on both sides so it sticks to
the lacquered wood as well as the vinyl. I guess time will tell if
it causes any damage...
